Moving heaven and earth for the eastern hellbender

By Naomi M. Klein | December 13, 2024

The eastern hellbender is a species of large slamander native to Missouri and nearby regions in the United States. Since the 1970s, their population has steadily declined due to a number of complications, including overhunting, climate change and mass infection from amphibian chytrid fungus. In 2021, the Missouri Department of Conservation partnered with the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service to pitch a plan to help them thrive, and just this year, in August, the full recovery plan was published. But why is it important, and what can we do to help?

To the east, in the streams

The image below describes the general habitat of the eastern hellbender in Missouri. Biologists like Trisha Crabill, an expert from the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, do not wish for the exact locations of the salamanders to be disclosed, as it encourages hunters and collectors to capture them from their homes. The salamanders live in freshwater streams mainly in the eastern and Ozark regions of Missouri, and their inclusion within them is vital to stream health.

The circle of life

Adult hellbenders lay clutches of transluscent eggs, which hatch into baby salamanders, called "larvae." The larvae reach their juvenile stage at about one year and continue growing for six to eight years, when they reach sexual maturity and can start the cycle over again.

Putting a plan into action

Over a course of 45 years, MDC and the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service will take and repeat these steps to ensure the hellbender population restores itself to near-historic numbers. The process, said Crabill, takes this long because the species takes so long to mature.

STEP 1: Propagation programs, such as the one at the St. Louis Zoo, will breed eastern hellbenders and augment them back into wild populations.

STEP 2: Experts will monitor the populations to assess their needs in the long term.

STEP 3: A watershed approach will be used to ensure habitats are safe and livable for the animals. More hands-on conservation efforts will be put into action.

STEP 4: The organizations will conduct more thorough research on the hellbenders to enhance conservation and recovery. Funding from other organizations, such as the Mark Twain National Forest Service, will aid in this step.

STEP 5: Scientists will monitor emerging diseases, such as amphibian chytrid fungus, and other stressors that could potentially harm salamander populations.

STEP 6: The organizations will push for policies and other measures to ensure the hellbenders are not disrupted, taken from their habitats or harmed in other ways by collectors and others who wish to harm them.

STEP 7: The organizations will work to educate the general public on hellbenders and explain their importance to our environment through outreach programs and other educational material.

What can we do to help?

The process will take a long, long time to complete. It is hard to say what exactly Missouri residents can do to help MDC and the Fish and Wildlife service in their efforts. But experts like Crabill and MDC director Jason Sumners say there are a few things we can do to be more mindful of our slimy friends.

The Mark Twain National Forest, pictured here, is dedicated to helping both the eastern hellbender and the Ozark hellbender. By visiting, you can assist in their efforts.

Just going out to visit the St. Louis Zoo can also help. Seeing a hellbender in person is a great way to experience them live and get to know what is being protected a little bit better, Sumners said.
